Pat Maddox hates blogging, but will do it anyway.

3 minute read

I love to write. I write basically every day. I don’t have a word count goal or anything like that… I’m just in the habit of writing.

Back in 2005 (!!), I started a blog to share my exploration of the FLPR stack – FreeBSD, lighttpd, PostgreSQL, and Ruby on Rails. I don’t know if anyone ever read it, or if anyone even used the stack besides me. I wrote mostly for myself though – because I enjoy writing, and because documenting my progress helps me learn.

Two years later, I decided FLPR was too niche and started a more general blog called “evang.eli.st – utterly unqualified opinions on software.” At the time, I had only had one programming job, which involved me converting a ColdFusion application to PHP. I didn’t enjoy the project itself much, but I worked with one of my best friends and got paid to program. I wrote my first blog post in the hopes that it would get me a job. It didn’t, but it did get me started on the path of writing and publishing regularly.

Eventually I got tired of awkwardly vocalizing my domain name, and decided I’d better just use my own name. I kept doing my thing, writing and publishing. I did that for over 10 years (!!) and now here I am… starting fresh.

I hate blogging, seriously.

Thanks for the history lesson, Pat… but why do you hate blogging?

Well, I’ll tell you.

I think it boils down to how my brain works, and how the internet works, which I think are basically the same thing. I get an idea (from someone else). I get another idea (from another someone else). I get more ideas. The ideas grow, and smash into one another, and change shape. They tug at each other, break, merge, multiply, and mutate. I don’t know how it happens, but it does, and I like it. I like it a lot.

Blogging doesn’t work that way, or at least has never worked that way for me. Blogging happens chronologically… I write a post on November 1st, and then another post on November 2nd. That’s how I wrote it, and that’s how you see it. But it’s not how I think, and that bothers me.

What happens when I think of something to add to the November 1st post, but now it’s November 3rd? Do I write a new blog post, and link back to the first one. Do I update the original post? If so, do I point out the update? What if you first read the post on November 5th, and never knew that I had updated it in the first place?

That’s a lot of questions for something that might be a trivial update. Whether I make a small change or a big change doesn’t even matter. The bigger problem I have is asking those questions in the first place. They create enough mental friction for me that I don’t want to deal with it.

I’m probably overthinking that stuff. I’ve been doing this long enough to know that I’m not going to stop. I need to address the problems I’ve identified. They get in the way of me writing and publishing, and that’s not cool.

My knowledge grows. My writing should grow with it.

The chronological paradigm of blogging poses an even bigger problem for me though. What if I don’t just want to add some details to a blog post… what if I want to represent what I actually know about a subject?

Content management systems have always had pages, which do the trick. In fact I’m pretty sure those existed long before blogs entered the Internet consciousness. That’s where I’m headed – getting back to basics, creating simple HTML pages.

A web page’s value can increase over time… by adding and removing content, restructuring the page, adding links. Maybe a single paragraph becomes a page, becomes a whole collection of pages. My thinking grows that way. I want my writing to grow that way too.

I have a way forward.

Fortunately, I have inspiration from Martin Fowler, who keeps a “bliki.” It’s a cross between a wiki and a blog, as the name suggests. I think he has the same sort of problems with blogs that I do:

“I find writing too hard to want to spend it on things that disappear” -Martin Fowler

To build my website again, I’ll use another thing I learned from Mr. Fowler: Refactoring. Put simply, my process from here on out is:

  1. write
  2. publish
  3. refactor

We’ll see how it works out. I suspect it will be pretty good.
