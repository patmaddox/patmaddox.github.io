So I’ll just dive right into it:

How old were you when you started programming?

I wrote my first program in second grade. My school had a lab of

Apple IIGSes, and the coolest teacher in the world, Mrs. Fletcher.

Mrs. Fletcher was this sparkplug of a woman with neatly kept hair that

constantly escaped to zany freedom. I think for the first week we

learned to draw or something. The second week, though, we learned to

make the turtle draw. By the way, when I say “first week” and “second

week,” I really mean “the smaller period of time at the beginning of

the year” and “the larger period of time at the end of the year,”

because in second grade I had no concept of time.

So here I am in the U-shaped computer lab directing a 5x5px turtle to

draw pictures of tanks and helicopters. Not real programming, but

that was the start. Mrs. Fletcher saw that I really liked

programming, and usually left me to my own devices to play with Logo

while the other kids had to follow her mostly-scripted lesson.

The next big step came when my teacher told my mom how interested I

was in computers. She explained that I wasn’t the typical kid who

liked to play computer games, but rather I took an interest in the

deeper, interesting “productive” aspects of computers. My mom,

totally kick-ass mother that she is, got me an oversized magazine

called (I think) “BASIC Programming for Kids.” I think it showed how

to start up QBasic and stuff, and had pages and pages of program

listings that I could type in to my computer at home. I don’t

remember it having any info on how to actually program, and if there

was then I never read it.

Being a pre-novice user, I had no idea I could save files. I used to

type 12 page-long programs into QBasic, repeating the exercise

nightly, due to the household mandate that the computer be turned off

before bedtime. You could say that I really dug

href=”http://codekata.pragprog.com/2007/01/code_kata_backg.html”>Code

Kata

.

I never used the programs I wrote much. As soon as I had figured out

the slew of syntax errors I made (long before I knew what a syntax

error was, of course) and gotten the program to run again, I would

immediately stop running it so I could make it do something different.

My favorite program was a blackjack game. My ultimate achievement was

prompting for the player’s name when the game started, and to deal

myself blackack every time and to deal everyone else random hands..

My mom thought it was great and encouraged my new hobby. Little did

she know that 10 years later, my interest in computers and gambling

would lead to me take an “extended” leave of absence from a $30k/yr

school that she was working hard to help pay for. More on that later.

How did you get started in programming?

We now know how old I was when I wrote my first program. I

didn’t really get started util 6th grade. This time it was a cool

Ex-pat teacher named Mr Bowyer. He introduced me to Java, after I had

told him that I really liked programming. Java was brand new or

something and was the future because you could put interactive shit on

webpages. It wasn’t that interesting to me. So when I went to the

bookstore with my mom to find a Java book, we came across one that was

about web programming in general. It showed you how to do stuff with

Java, C, Perl, and maybe some other things. I really liked the

spidering engine written in Perl. I was determined to write a spider

that would crawl every single page on the internet. Ah, if only my

mom didn’t make me turn off the computer at night, I’d have been king

of the internet and Larry and Sergey would have finished their PhDs

and lived in obscurity.

What was your first language?

Logo, I suppose.

The first programming language I ever tried to learn was C++. I had

that web programming book, and I deduced that Java was boring and C

was too old. C++ was smack dab in the sweet spot of

learn-worthiness. 12-year old logic rocks.

What was the first real program you wrote?

I don’t know if the others are answering this as “what’s the first

serious program you wrote.” I’m going to answer it as “what’s the

first program that you wrote without copying from a book.”

I had this obsession with writing programs that did things until

infinite. I already mentioned the web spider. Once I reached a point

where I could think logically, I wrote programs that counted to

infinite, found powers to infinite, calculated pi to infinite decimal

places, etc.

What languages have you used since you started programming?

I toyed with a bunch of different languages growing up. My mom made

me an agreement that she would buy me a computer book, and then when I

finished it she would get me another one. Her idea was that I was a

12 year-old, these are 500-page dry technical books, it won’t last.

As it turned out, I tore through these books in under a month and

sometimes even a week (of actual time, not little kid time). Computer

books being $50 a pop, my mom soon discontinued her offer and signed

me up for a library card instead.

My mom really supported my brother’s and my activities growing up. We

weren’t rich by any means, but she was willing to put a little bit of

money and a lot of energy into feeding our creative and intellectual

appetites. Both of our parents were great, actually. My dad used to

make balsa wood airplanes with my brother, though a big part of it was

just that he liked balsa wood airplanes - he didn’t “get” the

programming thing until much later (old fart doesn’t get computers

at all! :)

These days, people pay me to write programs, and my little brother is

beginning his first job at a regional airline. Go figure, noticing

your kids’ interests and then supporting the constructive ones goes a

long way.

Oh, wait. The question was what languages I’ve used. Logo, BASIC,

C++, C, Java, Perl, PHP. Those are when I was younger. More recently

I’ve used some for business and pleasure (Ruby & Javascript), some to

explore programming (Scheme, Smalltalk), some to tinker and make life

better (Emacs Lisp), and some to get over my intense fear of static

typing (Haskell & Scala).

What was your first professional programming gig?

Earlier I alluded to dropping out of school for computers and

gambling. Actually, I dropped out for other reasons, and I happened

to gravitate to computers and gambling.

I dropped out of school for three main reasons:

  • Rochester, NY is fucking cold and miserable
  • College without liberal arts or girls is not exactly educating me

    well for life

  • Building UML diagrams in Rational Rose and then generating

    skeleton code from them is a crappy way to program

College for me was a few fun-but-painful-as-in-hungover-not-emotional

memories of hanging out with smart, cool people who didn’t take life

too seriously. Unfortunately, the rest of the time was being

depressed about the weather, the boxes of never-to-be-used condoms my

mom sent me in care packages, and the fact that programming outside of

my door room was tedious and structured and just generally

spirit-killing.

Quick travel through time: I spent the next year and a half playing

poker in Bremerton and Seattle cardrooms. At some point I started to

play online. It was less fun, but I made more money.

I decided to move back home to Colorado, now that I could make money

playing online, instead of having to play in physical cardrooms. It

wasn’t long before I got bored, however, so I did some contract work

for a local web shop. The owner, Jim, was cool, and I met one of my

best friends doing it, but it was PHP and painful so I emailed Jim my

resignation at 2am one night I happened to win $200.

If you’re thinking I’m an irresponsible jackass at this point, well, yeah.

I started to play single-table tournaments, and I played multiple

games at a time. I learned that while most poker involves skills at

reading people, tournament end-games were largely mathematical. There

was a piece of software that would let you analyze hands and calculate

the precise amount of money you would win or lose depending on which

action you choose. It’s not really math that people can do in their

heads, what you’re really learning is situational awareness. I

decided to flip it around and write a “flash card” program - it would

generate scenarios, prompt me for an action, then calculate the

results of my action. It was a way to get a lot of experience

rapidly, and provided hook points to analyze poker problems even

further.

My friend saw this program and told me that I should turn it into a

web app and sell subscriptions. That was my first real Rails app, and

my customers paid me enough that I could maintain it a little bit and

get drunk with my friends a lot (it was paying the rent, too - at

least I was out of my mom’s house by this point!). I felt really cool

because rails was just starting up and 37signals was all like “you can

build products on the web!” and I had built a product that people paid

for, as my first commercial site.

If there is one thing you learned along the way that you would tell new developers, what would it be?

If you got into programming because you love doing it, then the most

important thing is to be mindful of those aspects of programming that

align with what you love. For me, the joy in programming comes from

ego and problem-solving. I was hooked on programming as an 8 year-old

because there was something in the world that I could control. I

lived an active little boy life, but when I made decisions that

conflicted with what adults wanted, I was always “wrong.” And here’s

the computer, a fancy expensive adult thing, that would *bend to my

will.* I also learned a little bit about responsibility - if the

computer didn’t do what I want, I couldn’t get angry or frustrated,

because it was just doing what I told it to. I had to take

responsibility for my emotions, and to choose to move ahead and solve

the problem at hand.

Besides ego, problem-solving is the biggest motivator for me. And

problem-solving to me isn’t necessarily completing a problem, but

rather reaching a point where I know the solution. I’m sure this is

why there are a lot of programmers just like me, who have dozens or

hundreds of half-baked projects sitting in their work/ dir. We only

manage to finish the ones that solve painful real-life problems. The

rest of them are cool ideas that get broken down into tiny

subproblems, and are discarded when the interesting subproblems have

been solved.

This is why I’d caution you to not take my home girl Amy Hoy’s words

too literally. You can say that programming is a means to an end, but

I think the journey is filled with endless nuggets that make you

happy. To be perfectly honest, I don’t see anything wrong with

arguing about syntax. I’m all for discussing the minutiae and

developing individual taste. It’s like anything else you might talk

about, important or trivial - you can probably have fun and/or learn a

lot, just don’t be an asshole.

I have a feeling that Amy would agree with some of that.

One key thing about being aware of your love of programming is to be

around other people who have that same passion and are doing cool

stuff. Friends of mine are building

, writing

(think emacs in Ruby - SICK). These kinds of people love to talk

about programming, and most of all illuminate creative areas that I

never knew existed.

If you got into programming for some reason other than you love doing

it - I dunno.

What’s the most fun you’ve ever had … programming?

Something recent was a combination of my ego and problem-solving

interests. I was talking with a friend about how to assign behavior

to arbitrary objects in predefined contexts. I decided it would be

easy to pop the existing methods off the object and store them away,

undefine the methods making it a blank slate, and point all calls to a new

object which had the stored methods bound to it. This had a

  • practical problem - adding and removing discrete units of

    behavior without modifying the object’s behavior

  • interesting subproblems - unbinding methods and rebinding them to

    other objects; reconstructing an inheritance heirarchy

  • ego trip - what kind of fucked up stuff can I do to Ruby? I had

    to take a trip into the C source to remove checks for

    class-equality that were probably important but were in my way so

    they had to go

My favorite program ever was something I wrote in tenth grade. I had

been dating the cute, smart soccer chick for a month. In high school,

a month is a big deal. To some people anyway. I don’t think my

girlfriend cared, but remember I was dating the cute, smart soccer

chick so I was counting my lucky stars. I wrote a program that

prompted her for her name, and when she entered hers it said “Happy

month anniversary, Christine.” If you typed something else it just

beeped at you. Fortunately she dug it, or dug me and just pretended

to dig it, so even at 15 or so lines of code it packed a lot of punch.

Lately I’ve been spending a lot of time building my emacs. God damn,

emacs is like crack to a tinkerer who spends most of his waking life

coding and working with text.
