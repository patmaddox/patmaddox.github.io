Over the past four days I’ve had the pleasure of discussing RSpec with several RubyConf attendees. I found a wide range of opinions. Several people are already on the RSpec bandwagon and use it daily. Others think it looks nice, but their team uses Test::Unit and they don’t have a strong motivation to switch. I didn’t ask Ryan Davis about it, fearing that he would ninja-kick me to South Carolina.

Things are looking good for RSpec in the near future. Eric Hodel, giving his productivity talk, asked the room who uses Test::Unit and who uses RSpec. I was amazed to see that it was split right down the middle. I don’t know what the numbers were like a year ago, but I have a feeling the majority of attendees didn’t even know what RSpec was.

David Chelimsky and Dave Astels presented RSpec in one of the Sunday morning talks. Anyone who talked with me during the conference can tell you how much I anticipated the unveiling of RSpec’s shiniest new feature, Story Runner. I must have said, “Story Runner kicks ass so much.(insert vague description here) - the Daves will show it off during their talk” a dozen times. When David demoed the plain-text story, the crowd applauded. They got it.

A lot of people already use and love RSpec. Many more have become interested in Story Runner’s potential. Still, there are a couple obstacles to ubiquitous adoption.

Stability is the first problem. I can’t count how many people told me, “RSpec looks great, but it just changes too often.” Ashley Moran, a well-known user on the RSpec mailing list, has described keeping up with edge RSpec as riding a roller coaster.

This isn’t a valid concern anymore, because RSpec’s core is stable. It took a while to get there, but we finally discovered the ideal syntax and structure and rolled it in to the 1.0 release.

I think the biggest problem surrounding RSpec is simply one of marketing. Many people’s concerns no longer hold true. Another example I found is that RSpec didn’t have nested contexts, one influence that led Tammer Saleh to write Shoulda. I remember there being plenty of list discussion on nested contexts. Ultimately David arrived on a far more elegant solution that fit RSpec’s approach - shared behaviors. It seems that people have concerns about RSpec that no longer hold true because they simply don’t know that things have changed for the better. I suspect that David and Aslak’s upcoming book will let people know that RSpec is ready for prime time.

The issue of stability surfaces again with the Story Runner’s introduction to the framework. It’s an exciting, powerful feature but is changing at a blinding pace. David posted to the list, announcing the first version of Story Runner, and a mere five days later he announced the plain-text story format. I know that many people are excited to check it out, but at this point it’s just impossible to keep up. Hopefully things will settle down in the next month and we’ll get to see some of the possibilities.

Another key obstacle is that several programmers or teams are heavily invested in Test::Unit. Ryan Davis brought up a related concern, saying that he “found comfort in def.” David said that he’s looking into writing a sort of compatibility layer that might ease the transition for Test::Uniters. I understand what Ryan means, but I don’t think RSpec needs to solve his problem. There’s an RSpec way, and if Ryan doesn’t like it then he doesn’t need to use it (and I’m sure he’ll happily agree with that). I’m interested to learn more about what David meant. I’m all for easing the transition from Test::Unit to RSpec. However I think that means writing tools that help you convert a Test::Unit suite into a RSpec suite. I don’t want Frankenspecs.

All in all, I’m excited about where RSpec is going. A lot of people are getting behind it, and I’m sure as we dispel myths then we’ll get even more support. We also need to put Story Runner through its paces and get it production-ready. Before Story Runner, I didn’t really want to sell people on RSpec, either it fit their thinking or it didn’t. Now though I believe that Story Runner provides a huge win and gives me a compelling argument to convert people to RSpec.
